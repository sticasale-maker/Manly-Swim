What we talked about

A short summary of the talk, to jog the memory or pass along to someone who missed it.

The Other End of Your Water connects the work of the Mermaid Pools Bushcare Group, at the top of the Manly Dam catchment, to the health of the ocean at Shelly Beach and Cabbage Tree Bay. Marco — a scuba diver who runs VIZ (Sydney Diving Visibility Reports) — explains that the creek the volunteers tend flows down through the suburbs into Manly Lagoon, one of the most polluted waterways in the state, and drains straight out to the sea where he dives. The bushcare group stands guard over the clean source, at the top of the tap.

Underwater visibility rises and falls with sediment. Every root kept in the ground, native replanted and bank held together keeps soil in place and the water clear. Once soil washes into the sea it's officially counted as pollution — and it harms marine life in four ways without ever being toxic:

☀️ Blocks light 🪨 Buries life 🚚 Carries pollutants 💨 Strips oxygen

Roads and roofs make storms flush the whole load out in one hit — a shock this coast never evolved to handle. So holding the soil on the hill keeps the light on, and the reef breathing, far below.

The talk also celebrates new science: environmental DNA lets researchers read a single bottle of water and list every animal that's recently passed through — already picking up warm-water fish arriving in Manly Lagoon as the sea heats. Two animals tie the worlds together: the eel, born at sea and raised in the creek, and the weedy seadragon, found nowhere on Earth but southern Australia's coast and utterly dependent on clear, sunlit water.

The closing thought: bushcare volunteers and divers are both citizen scientists — one in gumboots, one in flippers — watching patiently from hilltop to seabed. Twenty years of unglamorous work has left the water, and the sea, measurably cleaner.
