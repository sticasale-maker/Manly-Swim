The main screen

The three dots

Every forecast hour is scored three ways, because a swim has three different problems: getting in, getting across, and getting pushed around.

Each one is a dot on a rail. Left is good. Right is not. Tap any band below to see what it means in the water.

Two points, one bay

Where we measure

The app doesn't score "Manly". It scores two specific spots you actually swim at.

Entry is scored at the south-west corner of South Steyne, where most swimmers start. Chop and Surge are scored out on the line the Bold & Beautiful swim, roughly halfway to Shelly.

Under the hood

How the score is built

The app starts each hour with a clean slate, then takes points off for everything the ocean throws at it. Fewer penalties, better swim. Five things drive it:

Swell height

The biggest single lever. Energy climbs with the square of the height — a 1 m swell isn't twice a 0.5 m swell, it's four times.

Swell period

Seconds between waves. Long-period swell carries far more energy than short wind slop of the same height, and it's the part that surges through the bay.

Swell direction

The bay is a trap with one door. A southerly swell dies on the headland; an easterly walks straight in. The app runs the direction and period through a shelter table built for this bay's exact shape.

Wind

Offshore wind (from the west) grooms the surface and actually earns credit. Onshore wind builds chop over the fetch and costs you. Direction matters more than speed.

Tide

A multiplier, not a penalty. On a low tide the sand bank soaks up the waves. On a high tide the same swell arrives at your knees with nothing in its way — so a high tide makes everything else count for more.

Why the same swell scores differently at 6 am and 6 pm. Nothing offshore changed. The tide did. Swell height, period and direction set the raw energy; the tide decides how much of it reaches you.

What the numbers on the dots mean

Entry shows a number out of 60, counting up as conditions improve — a bigger number is a better swim. Chop and Surge just show where the dot sits on their own rail. Honestly: watch the colour and the word. The digits are for people who like digits.

Planning

Picking your window

The forecast runs hour by hour for the next five days. It's usually not "today is bad" — it's "today is bad at 11, and fine at 7."

  1. Scroll to your hour

    Tap any hour on the timeline and the three dots jump to that hour's forecast. The whole card updates — wind, tide, swell, the lot.

  2. Look for the run of green

    A single good hour surrounded by bad ones usually means the tide is turning under a big swell. Two or three green hours in a row is a real window.

  3. Check the tide against your swim length

    If you'll be out for 45 minutes on a rising tide with swell about, the exit will be harder than the entry. Score the hour you come out.

The rest of the screen

The other cards

Water temperature

Two sources sit side by side: the satellite sea-surface reading, and the temperature a real swimmer posted to the Bold & Beautiful group that morning. When they differ, believe the swimmer. Satellites measure the skin of the ocean; your shoulders are a metre down.

Water quality

Straight from Beachwatch NSW. It's worth a glance after heavy rain — the stormwater outlets don't care what the swell is doing.

Bluebottles

Reported by swimmers, for swimmers. If you get stung, or you see a windrow of them on the sand, log it. It takes ten seconds and it's the only bluebottle data anyone has for this bay.

Five-day outlook

Same model, run forward. Confidence drops the further out you look — treat day five as a hint and day one as a forecast.

What it can't see

The model is fed by offshore forecasts and a shelter table for this bay. It has never once looked at the water. It cannot see:

rips rocks sharks boat traffic the flags how you're feeling today

It's a forecast, not a lifeguard, and it is not a substitute for standing on the sand and watching a few sets go through. If the app says Easy and the water says otherwise, the water is right.

Swim with someone. Between the flags where there are flags, and never past your own limits because a dot was green.
Two taps

Put it on your home screen

Swim Manly is a web app — no store, no account, no ads, nothing tracked. Add it to your home screen and it opens like any other app, full screen, and loads even on a bad signal down at the beach.

  1. iPhone

    Open the site in Safari, tap the Share button, then Add to Home Screen.

  2. Android

    Open the site in Chrome, tap the ⋮ menu, then Install app or Add to Home screen.

Who made this

About this app

Swim Manly forecasts conditions for the swim between South Steyne and Shelly Beach, generated by our own wave-modelling engine.

Our goal

To help you make informed decisions before you head to the beach. This app is a completely non-profit, community-led initiative born from our shared passion for the Cabbage Tree Bay Aquatic Reserve. Whether you're a local or coming from further afield, we hope this tool makes your swim a little easier, safer, and more enjoyable.

Be patient as we tune the scores in the coming weeks and, as always, use your own judgment before entering the water.
